# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Financially literate individuals across three overlapping contexts:

- **Self-directed retail investors** doing their own research — building conviction before buying, checking if a stock is overvalued, validating or stress-testing a thesis
- **Amateur analysts and hobbyists** who want the tools professionals use, not dumbed-down screeners or broker portals
- **Small RIAs and advisors** running client due diligence without Bloomberg-level tooling

Primary job: run a DCF or reverse DCF on a stock, get a credible fair value estimate, and understand what the market is implying about future growth. Secondary jobs: compare securities, track portfolio valuations, screen by fundamentals.

Context: desktop and mobile, used when researching a position — not during active trading, not in a panic. Calm, analytical headspace.

## Product Purpose

Intrinsico is a DCF-based stock valuation platform. It gives financially literate investors access to institutional-quality discounted cash flow analysis, reverse DCF, fair value estimation, and fundamental screening — without a Bloomberg subscription, without a financial modeling background.

Success: a user opens Intrinsico, types a ticker, and within 60 seconds has a credible, model-backed answer to "is this stock cheap or expensive?" — with enough transparency to trust the number and enough depth to dig further.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, confident, opinionated.

Intrinsico has a point of view. It doesn't just surface numbers — it tells you what they mean. The tone is the smart friend who knows DCF cold: direct, precise, no hedging noise. Not academic. Not a data pipe. Not a trading app.

Voice: states conclusions, not disclaimers. Treats users as intelligent. Earns trust through transparency, not softness.

## Anti-references

Avoid every corner of the conventional financial product aesthetic:

- **Bloomberg Terminal**: Dense, unreadable without years of training, green-on-black or beige-on-gray. Unapproachable by design.
- **Traditional broker portals**: Dated, bureaucratic, 2005-era enterprise software. Heavy tables, legacy UI, no hierarchy.
- **Robinhood / retail trading apps**: Gamified, confetti-on-gains, casino energy. Color overload, animated tickers, chart fetishism.
- **Generic SaaS dashboard**: Metric cards, gradient text, identical card grids, tiny tracked uppercase eyebrows above every section, hero-metric templates. Could be any startup analytics product.

The challenge: every obvious direction in fintech is already taken by one of these four families.

## Design Principles

1. **The math speaks** — surface the numbers with clarity and confidence. Don't soften, hype, or hedge what the model says. If the stock is expensive at current prices, say so.
2. **Expert confidence, approachable depth** — feels like a smart analyst, not a PhD thesis. Dense when density serves the user; scannable when they're forming a first read.
3. **Information density without noise** — every element earns its place. No decorative chrome that competes with the signal. Hierarchy is used to direct attention, not fill space.
4. **Decisions, not just data** — always answer "so what?" Context, comparisons, and ranges that help a user form a view and act on it. Not a raw data dump.
5. **Trust through transparency** — show the work. Every output traceable to its inputs. No black boxes.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA as baseline. Financial semantics use color (green/up, red/down, amber/warn) — all color-coded states must have a non-color indicator (icon, label, or pattern) for color-blind users. Adequate contrast on both light content surfaces and the dark nav. Reduced-motion support on all animations.
